Sampling Ecosystems

DueAugust 3

Synopsis

Biological Sampling: You will use a randomly selected rectangular sampling area, or quadrat, to collect plants and animals from an ecosystem. After sorting the organisms by species, you will count the number of individuals, compare species richness with relative abundance, and calculate Simpson's biodiversity index. The index combines how many species are present with how evenly individuals are distributed among those species.

Comparing Ecosystems: You will select two ecosystem sampling sites, form testable hypotheses, and compare measurements from each site. Abiotic measurements include factors such as temperature, humidity, light intensity, and soil pH. Biotic measurements include species richness, biodiversity, plant biomass, and animal biomass. You will calculate differences between the sites and determine whether the collected evidence supports your original hypotheses.

Lab To-Do List

  • 1Begin with Sampling Ecosystems — Biological Sampling. Review the differences among a species, population, community, and ecosystem, and distinguish species richness from biodiversity.
  • 2Form a prediction about the characteristics of a highly diverse community. Consider both the number of species and the relative number of individuals belonging to each species.
  • 3Use the randomly selected quadrat to collect the simulated plant and animal sample. Sort the organisms by species and record the number of individuals in each group.
  • 4Calculate species richness and Simpson's biodiversity index using D = 1 − Σn(n − 1) / N(N − 1). In this version of the index, values closer to 1 represent greater biodiversity, while values closer to 0 represent lower biodiversity.
  • 5Explain why two communities with the same species richness can have different biodiversity values when one community is dominated by a single species.
  • 6Continue to Sampling Ecosystems — Comparing Ecosystems. Select two appropriate ecosystem sites and write hypotheses predicting how their abiotic and biotic measurements will differ.
  • 7Compare temperature, humidity, light intensity, and soil pH, then examine species richness, Simpson's biodiversity index, plant biomass, and animal biomass for the same sites.
  • 8When requested, calculate percent difference between site values using 100 × |A − B| / [(A + B) / 2]. Use the results to identify the largest and smallest differences between the ecosystems.
  • 9Evaluate whether the evidence supports each hypothesis and explain how the abiotic conditions may influence the community, biomass, biodiversity, and overall stability of each ecosystem.
  • 10Complete both conclusions and confirm that both lab submissions appear in Connect and sync to the Blackboard gradebook.
