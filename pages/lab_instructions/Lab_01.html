Why This Lab Matters

Biology is not just about naming organisms — it is about explaining how organisms are related. In this lab you will examine several kinds of evidence biologists use to study evolution: fossils, body structures, biochemical similarities, and evolutionary trees.

The main question: How do scientists decide whether similarity points to shared ancestry?

The Big Idea — Same Basic Bones, Different Shapes

One of the most important ideas in this lab is that different animals may have the same basic skeletal parts, even when those parts look very different.

A human arm, cat foreleg, whale flipper, and bat wing all share the same general forelimb pattern: an upper arm bone, forearm bones, wrist bones, hand bones, and finger bones. The exact shape, size, and function differ greatly, but the underlying pattern is shared.

You do not need to memorize the exact shape of every carpal, metacarpal, or phalanx. Focus on the pattern: the same basic bones are present, but they have been modified for different functions. A bat wing is a modified vertebrate forelimb with greatly elongated fingers supporting a membrane; a whale flipper is the same forelimb shaped for swimming. That shared origin is why these structures are homologous.

Important Distinction — Homology vs. Analogy

Homologous structures share a common evolutionary origin, even if they now perform different jobs. A human arm, cat foreleg, whale flipper, and bat wing are homologous because they follow the same underlying forelimb pattern.

Analogous structures perform similar jobs but do not come from the same ancestral structure. Bird wings and insect wings both allow flight, but they are not built from the same ancestral body part.

So the better question is not “Do these structures look alike?” but Did these structures come from the same ancestral structure, or did they evolve similar functions separately?

Background — Connection to Darwin

Darwin argued that living organisms change over time and that related organisms share common ancestry. Before modern DNA sequencing, scientists relied heavily on fossils, anatomy, development, geography, and observation.

Comparative anatomy was especially important. When scientists saw the same basic bone pattern repeated across vertebrate forelimbs, they read that pattern as evidence those animals inherited the structure from a shared ancestor. This lab connects directly to that tradition.

What Molecular Evidence Adds

Today, biologists can also compare molecules such as DNA, RNA, and proteins. Very similar sequences may suggest a more recent common ancestor; more different molecules may suggest a more distant relationship.

Molecular evidence does not replace fossils and anatomy — it adds another independent line. The strongest evolutionary explanations come from multiple sources pointing in the same direction.

Key Terms for This Lab

Evolution

Change in inherited traits in populations over generations.

Common Ancestor

An earlier organism or population from which later groups descend.

Homologous Structures

Structures with a shared evolutionary origin, even if shapes or functions differ today.

Analogous Structures

Structures with similar functions that did not come from the same ancestral structure.

Convergent Evolution

Evolution of similar traits in different lineages facing similar pressures.

Fossil

Preserved evidence of past life, used to study organisms that lived long ago.

Phylogeny

The evolutionary history or relationships of an organism or group.

Phylogenetic Tree

A diagram showing a hypothesis about evolutionary relationships.

Cladogram

A branching diagram showing relationships based on shared traits.

Node

A branch point representing a common ancestor where lineages split.

Character

A trait that can be compared among organisms — a bone, structure, protein, or molecular feature.

Molecular Evidence

Evidence based on molecules such as DNA, RNA, proteins, or biochemical similarities.

Reading Trees — A Quick Reminder

When reading a phylogenetic tree or cladogram, do not focus only on which organisms sit beside each other on the page — look for the most recent common ancestor.

Two organisms are more closely related if they share a more recent common ancestor. Branches can be rotated around nodes without changing meaning: the branching pattern matters more than left-right position.
